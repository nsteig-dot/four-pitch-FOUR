The Quasi-LBO Framework

Private equity returns follow a predictable decomposition: the business grows revenue, expands margins, and is eventually sold at some multiple of earnings. Along the way, free cash flow retires debt. The equity check compounds from two directions simultaneously — the business itself getting bigger, and the debt layer staying roughly fixed while enterprise value climbs above it. Every incremental dollar of EV creation flows disproportionately to equity holders. That mechanic doesn’t disappear in public markets. It just gets ignored.

The cleaner starting point is enterprise value, not earnings per share. Build a forward EBITDA projection. Apply a conservative exit multiple. Then work backward to equity value, netting out net debt after accounting for FCF accumulation and share count reduction over the holding period. P/E ratios are a snapshot of today’s interest burden — they don’t capture the quiet migration of value from the debt tranche to the equity tranche that plays out over years as cash flows accrue. That migration isn’t financial engineering. It’s arithmetic.

The important differences from a textbook LBO are worth naming. A public minority investor controls neither capital allocation nor exit timing. Without those levers, leverage is fragile rather than convex. So the conditions for this framework to work are specific: high-quality businesses that reliably compound free cash flow, sitting above a manageable and declining debt stack, trading at EV multiples that don’t price in the compounding. When those three conditions are satisfied, the equity payoff profile becomes structurally asymmetric — downside anchored to business quality, upside amplified by the math of value migration.

When the five questions get five affirmative answers, you’re not looking at a leveraged bet. You’re looking at something that resembles a small private buyout hiding in the public market at a discount. That’s not a reason to be aggressive — it’s a reason to be patient. The business compounds, FCF accrues, and the value follows. The weighing machine, given enough time, tends to respect the arithmetic.

The market’s entire bear thesis is one sentence: elevated leverage and an unproven acquisition. Both are temporary, measurable, and resolving on a calendar that is public knowledge.

Countering the “No Organic Growth” Argument

The bear claim that Shift4 has “no organic growth” is imprecise at best and demonstrably wrong at worst. It conflates the source of Shift4’s merchant base with the incremental revenue from deepening those relationships — two fundamentally different things. Five independent pillars dismantle this argument.

▶ Bear: Shift4’s growth is entirely driven by acquisitions — strip them out and there is nothing left.

Pillar I — Management Has Explicitly Disclosed Organic Numbers: FY2024 organic GRLNF growth was “well north of 25%” (Q2 2024 SEC filing). Q3 2025 organic growth: 18% (first explicit numerical disclosure). FY2025 excluding Global Blue & SmartPay: 23% GRLNF growth (Q4 2025 earnings call). FY2026 Americas guide — management’s own label for its “most mature” market with “minimal M&A annualization impact”: mid-teens GRLNF growth. The bear must explain away Shift4’s own disclosures.

▶ Bear: The “organic” label is misleading — those conversions come from acquired merchants, not genuine market share gains.

Pillar II — Gateway-to-E2E Conversion Is Organic by Every Financial Definition: Converting a gateway-only merchant to end-to-end processing generates 4–5× the per-merchant revenue on identical card volume at zero M&A cost (Stone Mountain Research). No new acquisition check is written. No new merchant is won. The conversion engine alone delivered $32.9B in E2E volume growth in the first 9 months of 2025 (Q3 2025 10-Q). Picolinie Capital framed this precisely: “That revenue may stem from past acquisitions, but it is now effectively organic — no further M&A needed to achieve it.”

▶ Bear: Future growth requires more acquisitions — the business can’t grow standalone.

Pillar III — $35B Signed-But-Not-Live Backlog Is Pre-Contracted Organic Revenue: As of Q3 2025, Shift4 held $35B+ of signed but not yet live merchant volume, up from $25B in Q2 2024. Management activated $6B+ in new volume in Q3 2025 alone. This pipeline requires zero new sales, zero new M&A, and zero capital to realize. Even if Shift4 stopped all acquisitions and new sales activity entirely, this backlog alone would fund multiple years of double-digit volume growth.

▶ Bear: SkyTab growth is just converting acquired merchant lists — not a real product business.

Pillar IV — SkyTab Is a 100% Internally Built Product at Near-Zero Penetration: SkyTab was built internally — zero acquisition cost. Subscription revenues: $341M in FY2024 (+90% YoY), $454M in FY2025 (+33% YoY), carrying no network fee pass-through. Over 55,000 installs since beta, far exceeding Shift4’s own 30,000 target. Restaurant and hospitality penetration remains in the low single digits. Toast, the closest comparable, serves ~127,000 locations. SkyTab’s organic runway is measured in multiples of current revenue.

▶ Bear: Even bulls admit organic growth is decelerating — from 20% to low double digits.

Pillar V — The Bears Don’t Actually Claim Zero Organic Growth: BTIG cut its target but kept a Buy, explicitly stating “predictions of minimal future growth overlook the numerous avenues available.” Raymond James downgraded only to “Outperform” (still bullish) at $67. Deutsche Bank — the most bearish voice — warned organic could fall from 18% to “mid-teens,” not zero. The worst-case consensus is low-double-digit organic growth on a $2.5B+ GRLNF base: that is $275–325M of organic revenue addition per year from the existing business alone, before any new M&A.

The Picolinie Capital Math — The Floor Argument: Strip out every acquisition-driven conversion entirely. Assume Shift4 never does another deal and loses all M&A-adjacent revenue growth. The payments industry still grows at GDP+. Shift4 is still a market share gainer with 98%+ merchant retention. Result: high single-digit “pure” organic growth floor (Picolinie Capital, Feb 2026). A business with high-single-digit organic growth, 49% EBITDA margins, $500M FCF, and a $35B signed backlog does not trade at a single-digit cash flow multiple. The discount is a sentiment artifact, not a fundamental verdict.

The Synthesis: Shift4’s acquisition playbook is its go-to-market strategy, not a substitute for organic growth. Every high-conviction acquirer — Constellation Software, Roper Technologies, Danaher — uses M&A as a distribution mechanism, then generates organic growth on top. The question is whether the organic engine works. Management disclosures, analyst consensus, and a $35B contracted backlog give the same answer: unambiguously yes.

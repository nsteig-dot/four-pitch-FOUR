# The Quasi-LBO Framework — User's Investment Philosophy Thread

## Core Concept
In private equity, returns decompose cleanly: revenue growth, margin expansion, exit multiple, and the cumulative impact of free cash flow used to pay down debt or repurchase equity. The equity check compounds not only because the business grows, but because leverage amplifies the value accruing above a relatively fixed debt layer. Every dollar of EV growth disproportionately benefits the equity holder.

The same framework applies to public equities — it's just rarely used.

## The Method
1. Start with enterprise value rather than earnings per share
2. Project EBITDA forward
3. Apply a conservative exit multiple
4. Bridge back to equity after accounting for cumulative FCF and share count changes

When you do this, certain businesses with meaningful but manageable leverage begin to resemble small buyouts already sitting inside the public markets.

## Why It Works
- If EV compounds and debt remains fixed or declines, the equity claim expands faster than the underlying business itself
- The market's fixation on point-in-time P/E doesn't capture this
- P/E reflects the current interest burden — it doesn't reflect the migration of value from the debt tranche to the equity tranche as free cash flow accumulates over time
- "This isn't financial engineering, it's structural math"

## Public vs. Private Equity Differences
- In a traditional LBO, the sponsor creates leverage at entry and relies on operational improvement and deleveraging to generate equity returns
- In certain public equities, the leverage is already embedded but the market prices the equity as though that convexity doesn't exist
- If the business is durable and the debt is survivable through a cycle, the equity holder benefits from the same mechanics

### Key Differences That Matter
- A public minority investor doesn't control capital allocation or exit timing
- Doesn't set the leverage level
- Must trust management to act rationally and steward the balance sheet conservatively
- Without that alignment, leverage becomes fragility rather than convexity

## The Checklist
The goal isn't leverage for its own sake. It's high-quality businesses that can compound FCF over long periods. When those businesses also sit above a manageable debt layer and trade at modest EV multiples, the equity profile becomes powerfully asymmetric.

- Downside is tied to business quality and balance sheet survivability
- Upside is amplified by the structural migration of value to equity over time

Five questions:
1. Is the business durable?
2. Is the growth self-determined?
3. Is the balance sheet safe?
4. Is management rational?
5. Does the current EV allow for an attractive 5-10 year IRR even without multiple expansion?

When the answers are yes, and leverage adds structural convexity rather than risk, the opportunity resembles a small buyout hiding in plain sight.

## Conclusion
"That's not a reason to be aggressive, but it's a very good reason to be patient. If the business compounds and FCF accrues, equity value follows. The weighing machine, in time, tends to respect arithmetic."
